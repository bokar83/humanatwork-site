The three questions HR asks before a restructure

Workforce planning meetings follow a pattern. These three questions get asked about every role when headcount decisions are being made. Most workers never know what they are. The workers who do, prepare differently.

Every restructure I have been part of, whether it involved 8 people or 800, moved through the same underlying logic. The specific roles, the timelines, the announcement language were all different. But the questions underneath the process were consistent.

These are not official HR frameworks with trademarked names. They are the questions that actually get asked in the room when a department head and an HR business partner are going through a list of roles together. I have been that HR business partner. This is what those conversations sound like.

The three questions, in order

What gets asked about your role in the room where you are not present
// QUESTION 01

Could this work be done without this person?

Not "is this person good at their job." Not "would we miss them." Could the work, specifically, be redistributed, automated, or eliminated? This is the replaceability question and it is the first filter. If the answer is yes with reasonable effort, the role enters the evaluation list regardless of individual performance. The person is not being evaluated yet. The role is.

// QUESTION 02

What is the consequence of removing this role in the next 90 days?

Not years out. Not in theory. In the next 90 days, what breaks, who feels it, and how visible is that pain to leadership? This is the consequence question. Roles whose absence would be invisible, purely internal, or easily distributed across existing teams are high risk. Roles whose absence would be felt by external stakeholders or revenue owners are at low risk. The time horizon of 90 days is deliberate. It is the planning window that drives these decisions.

// QUESTION 03

Would this person be difficult to replace if we needed to hire back in 18 months?

This is the optionality question. It cuts both ways. If the answer is "no, we could hire for this role in 30 days," the role is a cut candidate. If the answer is "yes, this person carries institutional knowledge or relationships we would struggle to rebuild," that is a protection factor. This is also where personal reputation, organizational visibility, and genuine subject matter expertise actually matter in the decision. Not performance reviews. Reputation.

Why these are different from performance questions

Most workers assume that performance reviews and restructures evaluate the same things. They do not. A performance review asks: how well is this person doing the job? A restructure asks: should this job exist? Those are fundamentally different questions and they require entirely different preparation.

A high performance rating does not protect a role that fails questions one and two. The question of replaceability and consequence is upstream of any individual assessment. Workers who understand this prepare differently from workers who assume strong performance reviews translate into restructure protection.

"A performance review asks how well you do the job. A restructure asks whether the job should exist."

How to move the answers in your favor

On replaceability (Question 01)

The goal is not to make yourself irreplaceable in a dramatic way. The goal is to ensure that your work is not so generic that it could obviously be automated or redistributed without disruption. Move toward judgment-intensive tasks: decisions, relationships, context that requires institutional knowledge. Work that is primarily sequencing, aggregating, or processing data is high risk regardless of how well it is done.

On consequence (Question 02)

Make the consequence of your absence visible to people with organizational authority. This is not about being loud or political. It is about ensuring that at least one senior person could articulate why your absence would matter, without having to look it up. If your impact is entirely internal and invisible above your direct manager, you are running structural risk regardless of how good your work is. The move is to generate external accountability, revenue connection, or cross-functional dependency around your role.

On optionality (Question 03)

Document institutional knowledge. Own relationships that would be genuinely painful to hand over. Operate in areas where the learning curve for a replacement would be steep and the ramp time would be costly. These are the things that make the 18-month question point toward protecting your role rather than cutting it. This is built over time, not in a week, but it is something you can actively shape.

The question nobody talks about

After the three questions, there is sometimes a fourth. It is not official. It does not appear on any workforce planning framework. But it surfaces in rooms where people are going through lists and the numbers are not adding up to the required reduction target.

The fourth question is: who on this list would we genuinely regret losing?

That question is answered almost entirely by reputation and visibility. You cannot build it in a week. But you can start building it the moment you understand it exists.
